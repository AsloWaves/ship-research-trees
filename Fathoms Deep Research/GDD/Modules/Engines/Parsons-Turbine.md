## Description

Revolutionary steam turbine design by Sir Charles Parsons that powered HMS Dreadnought and changed naval warfare forever. Uses direct drive without reduction gearing, requiring high propeller RPM. The smooth rotary motion eliminated reciprocating engine vibration, dramatically improving gunnery accuracy at high speeds.

- Smooth operation eliminates vibration
- Superior gunnery accuracy at high speed

- More compact machinery spaces

## Disadvantages

- No reverse capability (requires separate astern turbine)
- Inefficient at low speeds due to direct drive
- Poor fuel economy below 15 knots
- High propeller RPM causes cavitation
- Complex to manufacture initially
- Limited ability to operate damaged
- Early reliability issues during break-in

## Historical Notes

The Parsons turbine revolutionized naval power when HMS Dreadnought commissioned in 1906 with four turbines producing 23,000 SHP total. During trials, Dreadnought achieved 21.6 knots - fast enough to catch any existing battleship while maintaining accurate fire due to vibration-free operation.

Sir Charles Parsons' 1897 demonstration at Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee review, where his turbine yacht Turbinia outran Royal Navy patrol boats, proved the concept. The Admiralty initially resisted, but experiments with HMS Amethyst and HMS Velox demonstrated turbine superiority. By 1905, turbines were proven ready for capital ships.

HMS Dreadnought's turbines ran for 2,945 hours during trials and early service with remarkable reliability. The main limitation was poor efficiency at low speeds - cruising at 10 knots consumed nearly as much fuel as 15 knots. This problem was later solved by reduction gearing, but early Dreadnoughts accepted the limitation for the enormous tactical advantages.

All major navies rapidly adopted turbines after Dreadnought's success. The US Navy installed Parsons-type turbines in USS Michigan and South Carolina (1908), while Japan used them in Satsuma (1910). Within five years, no new capital ship was built with reciprocating engines.
