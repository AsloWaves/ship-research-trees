## Historical Background

The Type 93 torpedo, known to the Allies as the "Long Lance," was the most advanced torpedo of World War II. Its revolutionary oxygen propulsion system (using pure compressed oxygen instead of air) gave it unmatched range and left almost no wake - making it extremely difficult to detect and avoid.

The weapon was a closely guarded secret. Allied intelligence initially refused to believe that Japanese torpedoes could achieve such range and performance. The devastating losses at the Battle of Savo Island and other night actions were partially attributed to underestimating this weapon.

At 24 inches (610mm) diameter, it was significantly larger than contemporary Allied torpedoes (typically 21"/533mm), contributing to its exceptional range and warhead size.

### Handling Dangers
The oxygen propulsion system made the Type 93 extremely dangerous to handle. Accidental detonations from oxygen leaks caused several Japanese ship losses, including contributing to the loss of cruiser Mikuma at Midway.
