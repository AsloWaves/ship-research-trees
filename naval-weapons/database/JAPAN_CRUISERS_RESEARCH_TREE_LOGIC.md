# Japanese Cruisers Research Tree Logic

## Overview

This document defines the research tree structure for Japanese Cruisers (CA/CL) in the naval warfare system. Japan developed some of the world's most powerful and innovative cruiser designs, featuring exceptional speed, heavy torpedo armament (Type 93 "Long Lance"), and aggressive offensive capabilities. The **Mogami class** revolutionized cruiser design with its conversion from 15×6.1" light cruiser to 10×8" heavy cruiser, while the **Tone class** featured a unique all-forward turret arrangement optimized for reconnaissance.

**Node ID Range**: 4600-4642 (43 nodes)
**Nation**: Japan
**Ship Type**: CA (Heavy Cruiser) / CL (Light Cruiser)
**Time Period**: 1896-1960
**Tech Tiers**: 1-10

## Historical Context

### Armored Cruiser Era (1896-1912)

Following the Sino-Japanese War (1894-1895), Japan acquired armored cruisers to provide fleet scouting and commerce raiding capabilities. **Asama** and **Izumo classes** (British-built) provided the core of Japan's cruiser force during the Russo-Japanese War (1904-1905). These ships participated in the **Battle of Tsushima** (1905), where Japan's cruisers performed critical reconnaissance and screening roles. The **Tsukuba** and **Ibuki classes** represented Japan's transition to indigenous construction with increasingly powerful armament.

### Light Cruiser Development (1918-1925)

Post-WWI, Japan developed modern light cruisers to lead destroyer flotillas and provide fleet screening. The **Tenryū class** (1918)—Japan's first modern light cruisers—featured 4×5.5" guns and 33 knots, establishing the pattern for interwar designs. The **Kuma class** (5,500 tons, 7×5.5" guns, 36 knots) and **Nagara class** (similar) provided numerous ships for fleet operations. **Yūbari** (1923) was an experimental design by naval architect Admiral Hiraga Yuzuru—a compact cruiser testing new weight-saving techniques.

### Treaty Cruiser Era (1925-1936)

The Washington Naval Treaty (1922) and London Naval Treaty (1930) limited cruisers to 10,000 tons and 8" guns (heavy) or 6.1" guns (light). Japan pushed limits:

**Furutaka Class** (1925): Japan's first "treaty cruisers" with 6×8" guns. Rebuilt 1936-1939 with improved layout.

**Myōkō Class** (1927): Breakthrough design with **10×8" guns** (five twin turrets)—the most powerful armament on any treaty cruiser. Displacement: 11,300-13,000 tons (officially 10,000). Speed: 35.5 knots. Extremely aggressive design emphasizing offensive power over protection. All four ships (Myōkō, Nachi, Ashigara, Haguro) became legendary for their firepower and participated extensively in Pacific War.

**Takao Class** (1930): Improved Myōkō with better armor and AA. Four ships (Takao, Atago, Chōkai, Maya) featured distinctive bridge structure. Atago served as Admiral Kurita's flagship at Leyte Gulf.

### Mogami Revolution (1935)

The **Mogami class** represents one of the most innovative cruiser designs in history:

**Original Configuration (1935)**:
- **15×6.1" guns** in five triple turrets—the most powerful light cruiser armament ever
- Displacement: 8,500 tons standard (officially), 11,200 tons actual
- Speed: 37 knots (152,000 shp)

**After Treaty Abrogation (1939-1940)**:
- **Converted to 10×8" guns** in five twin turrets—transformed into heavy cruisers
- Added torpedo bulges increasing displacement to 13,670 tons
- Maintained 35 knots speed
- Retained 12× Type 93 "Long Lance" torpedoes (40 km range!)

This conversion capability was built into the original design—turret foundations could accommodate either 6.1" triples or 8" twins. The Mogami class demonstrated Japan's ability to circumvent treaty limitations through innovative engineering.

### Tone Class Innovation (1938)

The **Tone class** (Tone, Chikuma) featured revolutionary **all-forward turret arrangement**:
- **4×8" twin turrets** concentrated forward
- Entire aft section dedicated to **reconnaissance aircraft** (6+ floatplanes)
- Optimized for carrier task force scouting role
- Displacement: 11,200 tons, Speed: 35 knots

This unique design proved highly effective in carrier operations—Tone's aircraft discovered the US fleet at Midway, though the report came too late to prevent disaster.

### Wartime Development (1941-1945)

**Agano Class** (1941): Modern light cruisers with **6×6" guns** (twin turrets) and heavy AA armament (48× 25mm guns). Four ships (Agano, Noshiro, Yahagi, Sakawa) optimized for destroyer flotilla leadership. Fast (35 knots) but lightly protected.

**Ōyodo** (1942): Fleet flagship design with single triple 6.1" turret forward, massive command facilities, and 6 reconnaissance aircraft. Only one completed before resources prioritized to carriers.

**Special Conversions**:
- **Kitakami/Ōi**: Converted to **torpedo cruisers** with 40× Type 93 torpedoes (20 quad launchers) in 1941—the ultimate torpedo platforms
- **Isuzu**: Converted to **AA cruiser** (1944) with 8×5" guns and 66× 25mm AA guns after torpedo damage
- **Mogami**: Converted to **hybrid cruiser-carrier** (1943) after Midway—aft section replaced with flight deck for 11 aircraft. Unique emergency measure responding to carrier losses.

## Key Innovations

1. **Most Powerful Light Cruiser** (Mogami, 1935): 15×6.1" guns—unprecedented firepower
2. **Light-to-Heavy Conversion** (Mogami, 1939): Built-in capability to swap turrets from 6.1" to 8" guns
3. **All-Forward Turrets** (Tone, 1938): Revolutionary layout optimizing reconnaissance role
4. **Type 93 "Long Lance" Torpedoes**: 40 km range, 93% oxygen fuel—deadliest torpedo in WWII
5. **Torpedo Cruiser** (Kitakami/Ōi, 1941): 40× torpedoes—the ultimate torpedo platforms
6. **Experimental Weight Savings** (Yūbari, 1923): Admiral Hiraga's techniques enabling compact powerful cruisers

## Branch Descriptions

### 1. Armored Cruisers (10 nodes)

**Asama Class (1896-1898)**: British-built armored cruisers (Asama, Tokiwa) that formed the core of Japan's cruiser force during the Russo-Japanese War. Displacement: 9,700 tons, 2×8" + 10×6" guns. Both ships participated in the **Battle of Tsushima** (1905), providing critical reconnaissance. Asama grounded 1923, Tokiwa scrapped 1910s.

**Izumo Class (1899-1900)**: Improved British-built armored cruisers (Izumo, Iwate). Displacement: 9,750 tons, 4×8" + 14×6" guns. Both served at Tsushima. Izumo survived as training ship until scrapped 1945. Iwate survived WWII, scrapped 1946-1947.

**Kasuga Class (1902-1903)**: Italian-built by Ansaldo (Genoa). Two ships (Kasuga, Nisshin) purchased from Italy after Russo-Japanese War began. Unique Italian design: 10,960 tons, 1×10" + 2×8" + 14×6" guns. Both survived WWII as training ships.

**Tsukuba Class (1905-1906)**: First large armored cruisers built domestically. Displacement: 13,750 tons, 4×12" + 12×6" guns. Speed: 20.5 knots. Two ships (Tsukuba, Ikoma). Tsukuba exploded January 14, 1917 (magazine explosion, 305 dead). Ikoma survived as training ship, scrapped 1923-1924.

**Ibuki Class (1907-1909)**: Advanced armored cruisers approaching battlecruiser capability. Displacement: 14,636 tons, 4×12" + 8×8" guns, 21 knots. Two ships (Ibuki, Kurama). These represented the peak of Japanese armored cruiser development before the dreadnought revolution. Both survived WWI, Ibuki scrapped 1923, Kurama 1923.

### 2. Early Light Cruisers (10 nodes)

**Tenryū Class (1918-1919)**: Japan's first modern light cruisers designed for destroyer flotilla leadership. Specifications: 3,230 tons, 4×5.5" (140mm) guns, 6× torpedo tubes, 33 knots. Two ships (Tenryū, Tatsuta). Small but fast, these ships established the pattern for subsequent light cruiser development. Both sunk WWII (Tenryū December 1942, Tatsuta March 1944).

**Kuma Class (1919-1921)**: Improved design with **7×5.5" guns** and **8× torpedo tubes**. Displacement: 5,500 tons, Speed: 36 knots (90,000 shp). Six ships: Kuma, Tama, Kitakami, Ōi, Kiso, plus Nagara (reclassified). These ships provided the bulk of Japan's light cruiser force in the 1920s-1930s. Later, **Kitakami** and **Ōi** converted to **torpedo cruisers** (see special conversions).

**Nagara Class (1921)**: Refined Kuma design. Six ships with improved habitability and machinery: Nagara, Isuzu, Natori, Yura, Kinu, Abukuma. All served extensively in WWII. **Isuzu** later converted to AA cruiser (see special conversions).

**Sendai Class (1922-1923)**: Further improved light cruisers. Three ships: Sendai, Jintsu, Naka. Displacement: 5,195 tons, 7×5.5" guns, 8× torpedo tubes, 35.25 knots. **Jintsu** became famous for night battle at **Battle of Kolombangara** (July 13, 1943)—sunk by US destroyer gunfire after illuminating enemy with searchlights (+30% night combat bonus).

### 3. Experimental Design (1 node)

**Yūbari (1923)**: Revolutionary experimental design by Admiral **Hiraga Yuzuru**, testing weight-saving techniques that would influence all subsequent Japanese cruiser designs. Specifications:
- Displacement: 2,890 tons (very compact)
- Armament: 6×5.5" guns + 4× torpedo tubes
- Speed: 35.5 knots
- Innovation: Concentrated machinery, welded construction, efficient layout

This small cruiser demonstrated that powerful armament could be mounted on a compact hull through innovative engineering (+25% efficiency bonus). Hiraga's techniques were applied to Mogami and other designs. Yūbari served extensively in WWII, sunk by submarine April 28, 1944.

### 4. Early Treaty Cruisers (4 nodes)

**Furutaka Class (1925)**: Japan's first "Washington Treaty cruisers" limited to 10,000 tons and 8" guns. Original configuration: **6×8" guns in single turrets** (unusual single-gun mounts). Displacement: 7,100 tons (officially), 9,150 tons (actual). Speed: 34.5 knots. Two ships: Furutaka, Kako.

**1936-1939 Reconstruction**: All single 8" turrets replaced with three twin 8" turrets, improving efficiency and protection. Both ships sunk early WWII (Kako August 10, 1942 by submarine—first Japanese cruiser lost; Furutaka October 11-12, 1942 at Cape Esperance).

**Aoba Class (1926)**: Improved Furutaka with **6×8" guns in twin turrets** from the start. Displacement: 7,100 tons (officially), 9,000 tons (actual). Speed: 35 knots. Two ships: Aoba, Kinugasa. Both participated heavily in Guadalcanal campaign. Kinugasa sunk November 14, 1942. Aoba survived war heavily damaged, scrapped 1946-1947.

### 5. Myōkō Class (4 nodes)

The **Myōkō class** represents the breakthrough in Japanese heavy cruiser design—the most powerful armament on any treaty cruiser:

**Specifications**:
- Displacement: 10,000 tons (officially), 11,300-13,000 tons (actual)
- **Main Armament**: 10×8" (203mm) guns in five twin turrets
- Secondary: 8×4.7" + 8×25mm AA (initially)
- Torpedoes: 12× Type 93 "Long Lance" (40 km range!)
- Speed: 35.5 knots (152,000 shp)
- Range: 8,000 nautical miles at 14 knots

**Innovation**: Five twin turrets (3 forward, 2 aft) provided **the heaviest broadside of any treaty cruiser** (+40% firepower bonus, LEGENDARY). This aggressive design sacrificed protection for offensive power—thin armor but devastating weapons.

**Four Ships**:
- **Myōkō**: Participated in many engagements, survived war heavily damaged, scrapped 1946-1947
- **Nachi**: Famous flagship, extensive combat, sunk November 5, 1944 by carrier aircraft at Manila
- **Ashigara**: Known as "Hungry Wolf" by British, sunk June 8, 1945 by submarine
- **Haguro**: Distinguished service, sunk May 16, 1945 by British destroyers in last surface action

All four ships became legendary for their firepower and participated extensively in Pacific War. The Myōkō class established Japan's reputation for powerful cruisers.

### 6. Takao Class (4 nodes)

**Improved Myōkō** with better armor and distinctive bridge structure. Specifications:
- Displacement: 10,000 tons (officially), 13,160 tons (actual)
- Armament: 10×8" guns (five twin turrets like Myōkō)
- Improved armor: 127mm belt (vs. 102mm on Myōkō)
- Enhanced AA: 8×4.7" + 8×25mm (later increased to 96×25mm)
- Speed: 34.2 knots

**Four Ships**:
- **Takao**: Extensive service, survived war damaged, scrapped 1946
- **Atago**: Admiral Kurita's flagship at **Leyte Gulf**. Sunk October 23, 1944 by submarine USS Darter—Kurita barely escaped
- **Chōkai**: Distinguished service, sunk October 25, 1944 at Leyte Gulf
- **Maya**: Converted to AA cruiser (1943) with 8×5" + 48×25mm AA. Sunk October 23, 1944 by submarine

### 7. Mogami Class (4 nodes)

The **Mogami class** represents one of the most innovative and controversial cruiser designs in naval history:

**Original Configuration (1935)** - **Light Cruiser**:
- Displacement: 8,500 tons (officially), 11,200 tons (actual)
- **Main Armament**: 15×6.1" (155mm) guns in five triple turrets
  - **The most powerful light cruiser armament ever mounted**
- Torpedoes: 12× Type 93 "Long Lance"
- Speed: 37 knots (152,000 shp)
- Armor: Thin but innovative welded construction

**After Treaty Abrogation (1939-1940)** - **Heavy Cruiser Conversion**:
- **15×6.1" triple turrets replaced with 10×8" twin turrets**
- Added external torpedo bulges (increased protection)
- Displacement increased to 13,670 tons
- Speed reduced to 35 knots (due to bulges)
- **Conversion capability built into original design** (turret foundations)

**Four Ships**:
- **Mogami**: Flagship of CruDiv 7. Damaged at Midway. Converted to **hybrid cruiser-carrier** (1943) with aft section replaced by flight deck for 11 aircraft (desperate measure after carrier losses). Sunk October 25, 1944 at Leyte Gulf.
- **Mikuma**: Sunk June 6, 1942 at Midway—first Japanese cruiser lost to carrier aircraft
- **Suzuya**: Converted to AA cruiser. Sunk October 25, 1944 at Leyte Gulf
- **Kumano**: Survived multiple battles damaged, finally sunk November 25, 1944

**Revolutionary Impact**: The Mogami class demonstrated Japan's ability to circumvent treaty limitations through innovative engineering (+50% versatility bonus, REVOLUTIONARY). The built-in conversion capability was unprecedented in cruiser design.

### 8. Tone Class (2 nodes)

The **Tone class** featured revolutionary **all-forward turret arrangement** optimized for carrier task force reconnaissance:

**Specifications**:
- Displacement: 11,200 tons (officially), 13,000 tons (full load)
- **Main Armament**: 4×8" twin turrets—**all mounted forward**
- Secondary: 8×5" (127mm) HA guns
- Torpedoes: 12× Type 93 "Long Lance"
- **Aircraft**: 6+ reconnaissance floatplanes
- **Aft section**: Entirely dedicated to aviation facilities
  - Two catapults
  - Large hangar and maintenance facilities
  - Extended recovery area
- Speed: 35 knots (152,000 shp)

**Unique Layout Advantages** (+35% reconnaissance, +25% aircraft ops):
- All main battery could fire forward during high-speed approach
- Massive aviation facilities enabling extended reconnaissance
- Optimized for carrier task force scouting role
- No turrets aft simplified aircraft operations

**Two Ships**:
- **Tone**: Admiral Nagumo's reconnaissance flagship. **Tone's aircraft discovered US carriers at Midway** (delayed report contributed to Japanese defeat). Extensive service, sunk July 24, 1945 at Kure.
- **Chikuma**: Sister ship with identical role. Sunk October 25, 1944 at Leyte Gulf.

The Tone class proved highly effective in their intended reconnaissance role—a unique and innovative design responding to carrier warfare requirements.

### 9. Wartime Light Cruisers (2 nodes)

**Agano Class (1941)**: Modern light cruisers optimized for destroyer flotilla leadership and AA defense. Specifications:
- Displacement: 6,650 tons
- **Main Armament**: 6×6" (152mm) guns in three twin turrets
- Heavy AA: 4×3" (76mm) + 48×25mm (later increased to 61×25mm)
- Torpedoes: 8× Type 93 "Long Lance"
- Speed: 35 knots

**Four Ships**: Agano, Noshiro, Yahagi, Sakawa. Fast and well-armed for destroyer leadership, but lightly protected. **Yahagi** famous for accompanying Yamato on Operation Ten-Go (April 1945)—sunk with Yamato by carrier aircraft. All four ships lost in combat 1943-1945.

**Ōyodo (1942)**: Unique **fleet flagship** design combining command facilities with reconnaissance capability. Specifications:
- Displacement: 8,164 tons
- **Main Armament**: 6×6.1" (155mm) guns in two triple turrets (unusual—one forward, one aft)
- Aircraft: 6 reconnaissance floatplanes
- Massive command and communications facilities
- Speed: 35 knots

**Only one completed** before resources prioritized to carriers. Served as Combined Fleet flagship 1943-1944. Survived WWII damaged, scrapped 1946-1947. The Ōyodo represented Japan's final cruiser development before material shortages halted construction.

### 10. Late War Designs (2 nodes)

**Ibuki (WWII)**: Improved heavy cruiser design begun 1942. Specifications:
- Displacement: 12,200 tons
- Armament: 10×8" guns (five twin turrets)
- Enhanced AA: 8×3.9" + 96×25mm
- Speed: 34 knots

**Construction**: 80% complete when work halted November 1943. Japan prioritized carriers, converting Ibuki to light carrier (never completed). The Ibuki represented the ultimate evolution of Japanese heavy cruiser design incorporating all lessons learned, but wartime priorities prevented completion.

**Future Cruiser**: Theoretical post-war design. Japan prohibited from major warships by occupation. No cruiser program pursued post-1945.

## Special Conversions

### Kitakami & Ōi - Torpedo Cruisers (1941)

The ultimate torpedo platforms—an extreme expression of Japan's torpedo doctrine:

**Conversion** (Kuma-class light cruisers):
- **40× Type 93 "Long Lance" torpedoes** in 20 quadruple launchers
  - 10 launchers per side
  - Reloadable from magazines
- Main guns reduced to 4×5.5"
- Purpose: Overwhelming torpedo attack in decisive fleet battle
- **Most heavily armed torpedo warships ever built** (+100% torpedo capability)

**Reality**: The decisive fleet battle doctrine never materialized. Both ships reconverted:
- **Kitakami**: Reconverted to fast transport (1942), kaiten carrier (1944)
- **Ōi**: Torpedoed by submarine USS Flasher, July 19, 1944

**Historical Significance**: These conversions represented the extreme application of Japan's offensive torpedo doctrine—prioritizing devastating first strike over balanced capability.

### Isuzu - AA Cruiser (1944)

Desperate conversion responding to overwhelming US air superiority:

**Conversion** (Nagara-class light cruiser damaged by torpedo 1944):
- **Main Battery**: 8×5" (127mm) HA guns in four twin mounts
- **Close-range AA**: 66×25mm guns in triple mounts
  - The heaviest AA armament on any Japanese cruiser
- All torpedo tubes removed
- Enhanced fire control and radar
- **Purpose**: Provide heavy AA defense for convoys and task forces (+80% AA capability)

**Service**: Commissioned September 1944 with new armament. Sunk April 7, 1945 by submarine USS Gabilan—the AA conversion came too late to significantly impact the war.

### Mogami - Hybrid Cruiser-Carrier (1943)

Emergency response to carrier losses at Midway:

**Conversion**:
- Aft turrets (4-5) removed
- 70-meter flight deck installed aft
- Hangar for 11 aircraft
- Forward armament retained: 5×8" guns in three turrets
- Unique combination of cruiser firepower and carrier aviation

**Reality**: Aircraft never embarked. Mogami operated as heavy cruiser with reduced armament. Sunk October 25, 1944 at Leyte Gulf before aircraft operations could be tested. The conversion demonstrated Japan's desperation after Midway but proved ineffective.

## Gameplay Notes

### Balance Considerations

1. **Myōkō as Legendary Design**: 10×8" guns—most powerful treaty cruiser (+40% firepower)
2. **Mogami's Versatility**: Convertible 15×6.1" → 10×8" design (+50% versatility bonus, REVOLUTIONARY)
3. **Tone's Unique Role**: All-forward turrets optimized for reconnaissance (+35% recon)
4. **Type 93 Torpedoes**: 40 km range—deadliest torpedo in game (+60% effectiveness)
5. **Special Conversions**: Kitakami (40× torpedoes), Isuzu (66×25mm AA), Mogami (hybrid)
6. **Paper Ship Penalty**: Ibuki 80% complete, reliability penalty
7. **Cost Scaling**: Heavy cruisers expensive (Myōkō $8M, Mogami $12M)

### Japanese Cruiser Characteristics

**Strengths**:
- **Firepower**: Most heavily armed treaty cruisers (Myōkō 10×8" guns)
- **Torpedoes**: Type 93 "Long Lance" with 40 km range (+60% effectiveness)
- **Speed**: Fast cruisers (34-37 knots) enabling aggressive tactics
- **Innovation**: Mogami conversion capability, Tone all-forward turrets
- **Night Combat**: Excellent optics and training (+25% accuracy at night)

**Weaknesses**:
- **Protection**: Thin armor prioritizing offense over defense (-15% survivability)
- **Stability**: Top-heavy designs (especially Myōkō/Takao with heavy superstructures)
- **Cost**: Expensive heavy cruisers reflecting complexity
- **Late Bloom**: Peak capability mid-tree (Tier 8-9)
- **Losses**: All heavy cruisers lost in WWII except Myōkō and Takao

### Strategic Role

**Fleet Screening**: Cruisers provided advanced scouting, especially Tone class with 6+ aircraft.

**Night Combat**: Japanese cruisers excelled at night surface combat using excellent optics and Type 93 torpedoes. Famous night battles: Savo Island (August 9, 1942—four Allied cruisers sunk), Kolombangara (July 13, 1943—Jintsu sunk but Allied cruiser damaged).

**Convoy Escort/Attack**: Light cruisers led destroyer flotillas in convoy operations and anti-shipping strikes.

**Carrier Escort**: Fast heavy cruisers (Takao, Tone classes) provided AA defense and surface protection for carrier task forces.
