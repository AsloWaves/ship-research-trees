**Small Warriors** - Mutsuki-class were 1920s-era small destroyers. 12 ships built 1926-1927. Four 4.7-inch guns + six 24-inch torpedoes. High speed (37 knots). Smallest IJN destroyers in WWII. All 12 saw combat - 9 lost in action, 3 survived war but scrapped. Kikuzuki first IJN destroyer sunk by aircraft (Tulagi, May 1942).

**Key Specs:** 1,315/1,800 tons | 335ft | 37 kn | 4× 120mm + 6× 610mm torpedoes | Unarmored | 12 ships

**Innovation:** Smaller than Special Type destroyers (Fubuki, etc.). High speed (37 knots) for size. Last IJN destroyers with 4.7-inch guns (subsequent classes had 5-inch). Six 24-inch torpedoes in two triple mounts. Light and fast for 1920s. Obsolete by WWII standards but all 12 pressed into combat. Showed even small, old destroyers were valuable. Kikuzuki achieved dubious first - first IJN destroyer sunk by aircraft alone.

**Design:** Four single 4.7-inch guns (two forward, two aft). Two triple 24-inch torpedo launchers. High speed (37 knots) despite small size. Light displacement (1,315 tons standard). Small crew (150). Good for 1920s, inadequate by 1940s. Light AA armament. Depth charges for ASW. Unarmored. Simple, economical design.

**Major Operations:**

**Battle of Wake Island (December 11, 1941) - Kisaragi:**
*First US victory of WWII*

**Background:**
- Japanese invasion of Wake Island
- US Marine defense with 4 F4F Wildcats

**December 11, 1941:**
- Kisaragi approaching Wake to land troops
- Attacked by Marine F4F Wildcats
- Hit by bombs from Captain Elrod's aircraft
- **Magazine explosion**
- **Broke in two and sank**
- All hands lost (~175 crew)
- **First Japanese destroyer sunk in WWII**
- **First IJN ship sunk by US aircraft**

**Battle of Tulagi (May 3-4, 1942) - Kikuzuki:**
*First destroyer sunk by carrier aircraft*

**Background:**
- Japan occupied Tulagi (near Guadalcanal)
- USS Yorktown launched air strikes

**May 4, 1942:**
- Kikuzuki at anchor in Tulagi harbor
- Attacked by SBD Dauntless dive bombers from Yorktown
- Hit by bombs
- Beached to avoid sinking
- Continued air attacks destroyed ship
- **First IJN destroyer sunk by carrier aircraft**

**Guadalcanal Campaign:**

**August 25, 1942 - Mutsuki:**
- Tokyo Express run to Guadalcanal
- Attacked by B-17 bombers
- Hit by bombs, sank
- High casualties

**Battle of Bismarck Sea (March 1-3, 1943) - Mikazuki:**
- Escorting troop convoy to New Guinea
- Massive Allied air attack (340+ aircraft)
- Mikazuki hit by bombs and strafing
- Sank March 1, 1943
- Convoy disaster - all transports sunk

**Battle of Kula Gulf (July 5-6, 1943) - Nagatsuki:**
- Tokyo Express mission
- Night action against US cruisers/destroyers
- Nagatsuki ran aground during evasive maneuvers
- **Grounded at full speed**
- Abandoned, later destroyed by Allied aircraft
- One of few IJN ships lost to grounding in combat

**Loss Statistics:**
- **12 built, 9 lost, 3 survived**
- **Causes:**
  - Air attack: 8 ships (89% of losses)
  - Grounding: 1 ship (11% of losses)
- **Loss period:** December 1941 - June 1944 (31 months)
- **Survival rate:** 25% (3 of 12)

**Why Heavy Air Losses:**
1. **Small size:** Less AA armament (only 4× 25mm)
2. **Slow to maneuver:** Compared to larger destroyers
3. **Old design:** Built 1926, no modern radar/fire control
4. **Vulnerable:** Light construction, no armor
5. **Air power dominance:** By 1942-1944, aircraft ruled Pacific

- **Muzzle velocity:** 825 m/s (2,707 ft/s)
- **Maximum range:** 15,000 meters (16,400 yards)
- **Rate of fire:** 8-10 rounds per minute

**Why Used in WWII Despite Age:**
1. **All hulls needed:** Every destroyer valuable
2. **Convoy escort:** Suitable for secondary duties
3. **Tokyo Express:** Fast enough for supply runs
4. **ASW:** Depth charges for submarine hunting
5. **Transport:** Could carry troops/supplies

**Tactical Employment:**
- **Tokyo Express:** Guadalcanal supply runs
- **Convoy escort:** Merchant ship protection
- **ASW patrols:** Submarine hunting
- **Transport:** Fast troop/supply transport
- **Screening:** Carrier/battleship escorts (less common)

**Modernization Attempts (1930s-1940s):**
- AA armament increased: 4× to 20+ 25mm guns (some ships)
- Depth charges increased: 18 to 36
- Radar added (some ships, late war)
- But fundamentally limited by size and age

**Legacy:** Mutsuki-class represented 1920s destroyer design - small, fast, economical. Obsolete by WWII but all 12 pressed into service. 9 of 12 lost in combat (75% loss rate). Eight lost to air attack - showed vulnerability of small, lightly-armed ships to aircraft. Kisaragi first IJN destroyer sunk (Wake Island). Kikuzuki first sunk by carrier aircraft (Tulagi). Three survivors (25%) - Mochizuki, Uzuki, Yuzuki - showed some luck existed. Small but served courageously in impossible circumstances. Names: Lunar months in Japanese calendar.

**Historical Significance:** Mutsuki-class showed even obsolete destroyers were used until destroyed. Built 1926, fought 1941-1944 in modern war. 75% loss rate demonstrated vulnerability. Air power killed 89% - epitomized shift from surface to air-dominated warfare. Kisaragi at Wake Island first victory for US - morale boost. Kikuzuki at Tulagi first carrier aircraft kill - foreshadowed future. Small size (1,315 tons) insufficient for WWII, but IJN had no choice - every hull needed. Three survivors showed luck mattered - avoided major battles, survived. Represented generation of ships built for different era, destroyed in new reality.
