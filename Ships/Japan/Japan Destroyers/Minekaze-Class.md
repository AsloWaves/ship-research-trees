**Fastest Destroyers of 1920s** - Minekaze-class were Japan's first post-WWI destroyers. 15 ships commissioned 1920-1922. Revolutionary speed: 39 knots (fastest destroyers in world when built!). Set speed records for destroyers. 1,650 tons, 4× 120mm guns, 6× 21-inch torpedoes. Designed for high-speed scouting and torpedo attacks. Bridge between WWI designs and revolutionary Fubuki-class. All 15 fought entire WWII. 13 lost (86.7% casualty rate). 2 survivors: Namikaze, Hakaze. Minekaze-class: Speed demons of 1920s, paid heavy price in WWII.

**Key Specs:** 1,215/1,650 tons | 336ft | 39 kn | 4× 120mm + 6× 21-inch torpedoes | 15 ships | 13 lost

**Innovation:** Revolutionary speed for early 1920s (39 knots!). **Fastest destroyers in world** when commissioned (held record until Fubuki). High-pressure oil-fired boilers (38,500 shp, 2 shafts). Lightweight construction (1,650 tons full). Four 120mm Type 3 guns (single mounts, good arcs). Six 533mm (21-inch) torpedo tubes (two triple mounts - heavy torpedo armament). Long, sleek hull (336 ft). Excellent seakeeping. Designed for: High-speed scouting, torpedo attacks, fleet screening. Influenced by WWI experience (need for speed in destroyer actions). First-class destroyers (larger than second-class). Bridge design: Compact, destroyer leaders. Set standards for Japanese destroyer development. Lessons learned → Kamikaze-class → Fubuki-class (revolutionary Special Type). 15 ships built rapidly (1920-1922 - 2 years!). War-tempo construction continued from WWI.

**Design:** Long, narrow destroyer hull (336 ft, 1,650 tons). Four 120mm (4.7-inch) Type 3 guns: single mounts (2 forward, 2 aft). Open mounts (no shields, crews exposed). Six 21-inch torpedo tubes: two triple mounts (centerline). Type 8 torpedoes initially (later Type 93 Long Lance refit possible). Two 7.7mm machine guns (minimal AA - 1920s design). Two funnels (raked). Three Kampon oil-fired boilers (high-pressure, modern). Steam turbines (38,500 shp - powerful for size). Two shafts. **39 knots max speed** (exceptional!). Range 3,600 nm at 14 knots (adequate). Crew 148 (small for size - efficiency). 18 depth charges (ASW capability). Mine rails (could lay mines). Lightweight construction (speed emphasized over armor/protection). Fine hull lines (minimize drag). Beautiful profile (sleek, fast-looking).

**Ships (15 built - 13 lost, 2 survived):**

**All Commissioned 1920-1922:**

1. **Minekaze** - Commissioned May 29, 1920
   - Lead ship, name means "Peak Wind"
   - First post-WWI Japanese destroyer
   - Set speed records (39+ knots trials)
   - Early WWII: Philippines, Dutch East Indies
   - Lost: February 10, 1944, Ormoc Bay (Philippines)
   - Sunk by US aircraft
   - 61 killed

2. **Sawakaze** - Commissioned September 30, 1920
   - "Refreshing Wind"
   - Early war operations
   - Lost: June 12, 1944, off Tawitawi
   - Torpedoed by US submarine Harder (famous submarine, sank multiple destroyers)
   - 122 killed

3. **Okikaze** - Commissioned November 30, 1920
   - "Open Sea Wind"
   - Pearl Harbor strike force (escorted carriers)
   - Lost: November 25, 1943, Cape St. George (Solomons)
   - Sunk by US destroyers (night surface action)
   - 178 killed

4. **Shimakaze** - Commissioned May 30, 1920
   - "Island Wind"
   - Early war: Philippines, Aleutians
   - Lost: January 12, 1944, near Rabaul
   - Sunk by US aircraft
   - 69 killed

5. **Nadakaze** - Commissioned December 28, 1920
   - "Open Field Wind"
   - Early WWII service
   - Lost: July 5, 1943, Kula Gulf (Solomons)
   - Sunk by US cruiser gunfire (night battle)
   - Heavy casualties

6. **Yakaze** - Commissioned December 10, 1920
   - "Arrow Wind"
   - China operations, early WWII
   - Lost: August 25, 1942, Santa Isabel Island (Solomons)
   - Sunk by US aircraft
   - Early loss

7. **Hakaze** - Commissioned January 16, 1920
   - "Wave Wind"

   - **SURVIVED WAR** (one of 2 survivors)
   - Post-war: Reparations to UK (transferred)
   - Renamed HMS Kempston (brief British service)
   - Scrapped 1947

8. **Shiokaze** - Commissioned May 15, 1921
   - "Tide Wind"
   - Early WWII operations
   - Lost: September 18, 1943, near Rabaul
   - Torpedoed by US submarine S-44
   - 141 killed

9. **Akikaze** - Commissioned December 16, 1920
   - "Autumn Wind"

   - Lost: November 13, 1944, Manila Bay

10. **Yukaze** - Commissioned May 28, 1922
    - "Evening Wind"

11. **Tachikaze** - Commissioned November 15, 1921
    - "Rising Wind"

    - "Field Wind"

14. **Namikaze** - Commissioned December 28, 1920

**86.7% Loss Rate:**

**High Attrition:**
- 15 ships built
- 13 lost in action (86.7%)
- 2 survivors

**Causes:**
- **Aircraft**: 9 ships (60% of losses - air power dominant)
- **Submarines**: 4 ships (27%)
- **Surface combat**: 2 ships (gunfire, night battles)

**Why So High:**
1. **Old ships**: Built 1920-1922, 20+ years old by WWII (worn out)
2. **Light construction**: Designed for speed, minimal armor
3. **Entire war service**: All 15 fought 1941-1945 (maximum exposure)
4. **Obsolete**: 1920s design vs 1940s threats (aircraft, radar)
5. **No early retirement**: Needed every destroyer (fleet shrinking)
6. **Aircraft vulnerability**: No adequate AA defense (1920s design)
7. **Submarine threat**: Pacific became US submarine hunting ground 1943-1945

**39-Knot Speed Record:**

**Revolutionary (1920):**
- Minekaze: 39 knots trials
- **Fastest destroyers in world** when commissioned
- Previous record: ~35 knots (WWI British/US destroyers)
- Held speed crown until Fubuki (1928, 38 knots)
- Actually maintained higher speed (Fubuki rated 38, Minekaze 39)

**How Achieved:**
- High-pressure boilers (38,500 shp on 1,650 tons = high power-to-weight)
- Lightweight construction (minimal armor, light fittings)
- Fine hull lines (long, narrow - low drag)
- Efficient turbines

**Significance:**
- Showed Japanese commitment to speed
- Influenced destroyer doctrine (fast fleet operations)
- Speed emphasized over protection (Japanese philosophy)
- Proved speed achievable with oil-fired boilers

**By WWII:**
- 39 knots still respectable (many WWII destroyers 33-36 knots)
- But 20+ year old machinery worn
- Actual speeds likely lower (35-37 knots realistic by 1944)

**Historical Significance:** Minekaze-class represented Japan's post-WWI destroyer philosophy: Extreme speed above all. 39 knots (1920!) faster than any destroyer in world - held record into late 1920s. Shows Japanese emphasis on aggressive fleet tactics (high-speed scouting, torpedo attacks). Influenced design evolution: Minekaze (39 kn) → Kamikaze (37 kn, more refined) → Fubuki (38 kn, enclosed turrets, revolutionary). Contrast with US: Clemson-class (156 ships 1919-1922) had low WWII losses - better construction, US air superiority, less exposure. Minekaze-class: Set speed standards, pioneered modern destroyer design, paid ultimate price. Remembered as fastest destroyers of their era.
