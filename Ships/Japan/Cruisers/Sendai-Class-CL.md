**Final 5,500-Ton Type** - Sendai-class were the last 5,500-ton light cruisers. 3 ships commissioned 1924-1925. Most refined Kuma/Nagara/Sendai design. Improved AA armament. Same specs: 5,832 tons, 35.5 knots, 7× 140mm guns, 8× 24-inch Long Lance torpedoes. Destroyer flotilla leaders. All 3 fought entire WWII. **All 3 lost in 1943** (100% casualty rate). **All lost within 2 months** (November-December 1943!). Famous battles: Sendai at Empress Augusta Bay (November 2, 1943, night action), Jintsuu at Kolombangara (July 13, 1943, heroic last stand), Naka at Truk (February 17, 1944, carrier raid). Sendai-class: Final evolution of successful design, complete sacrifice in 1943.

**Key Specs:** 5,195/5,832 tons | 535ft | 35.5 kn | 7× 140mm + 8× 24-inch torpedoes | 3 ships | **ALL 3 LOST 1943**

**Innovation:** Final and most refined 5,500-ton light cruiser (1924-1925). **Improved AA** - better positions, more guns than earlier classes. **Refined bridge** - lessons from Kuma and Nagara incorporated. **Better fire control** - Type 14 system optimized. Same basic design: 35.5 knots (slightly slower than 36 kn earlier ships, but more reliable). 7× 140mm guns (single mounts). 8× 610mm Type 93 Long Lance torpedoes (oxygen-fueled, 40,000-yard range). Light armor (2.5-inch belt, 1.25-inch deck). Seaplane catapult amidships (1 floatplane). Four funnels. Destroyer flotilla leader role (scout, lead torpedo attacks, escort). Only 3 ships built (smallest 5,500-ton class: vs Kuma 5, Nagara 6). End of 5,500-ton production (14 total: Kuma 5 + Nagara 6 + Sendai 3). Next light cruisers: Agano-class 1942 (different design, larger, more modern).

**Design:** Sleek light cruiser hull (535 ft, 5,832 tons). Seven 140mm Type 3 guns: single open mounts (3 forward, 2 midships, 2 aft). Good arcs, exposed crews. Eight 24-inch torpedo tubes: four twin mounts. Type 93 Long Lance: deadliest torpedoes in world (40 km range, oxygen engine, wakeless, 490 kg warhead). Two 80mm AA guns initially, increased to 8× 25mm by WWII (still inadequate). Improved bridge (larger, better visibility than Kuma). Four funnels (distinctive, easily recognized). Light armor: 2.5-inch belt (minimal protection, speed prioritized). 1.25-inch deck. Twelve Kampon oil-fired boilers. Steam turbines (90,000 shp). Four shafts. 35.5 knots (very fast for 1920s). Range 5,000 nm at 14 knots. Crew 450. Seaplane catapult: 1 Type 95 or Type 0 reconnaissance floatplane. Minelaying rails (48 mines). Refined but same philosophy: Speed and offense over defense.

**Ships (3 built - ALL 3 LOST):**

1. **Sendai** - Commissioned April 29, 1924
   - Lead ship, name means "Sendai" (city in northern Japan)
   - Early war: Philippines, Dutch East Indies, Solomons
   - Guadalcanal campaign (1942-1943)
   - Famous action: **Battle of Empress Augusta Bay** (November 2, 1943)
     - Night surface battle (Bougainville, Solomons)
     - Japanese force: Sendai + destroyers vs US cruisers/destroyers
     - Sendai engaged US force (radar vs visual fight)
     - **Hit by multiple US cruisers** (Helena, others)
     - 6-inch and 5-inch shells devastated light cruiser
     - Sendai exploded, sank rapidly
     - **Captain and most crew killed** (~330 dead)
   - Lost: November 2, 1943
   - First Sendai-class lost

2. **Jintsuu** - Commissioned July 31, 1925
   - "Jintsuu River"
   - Last Sendai-class completed
   - Early war: Philippines, Dutch East Indies, Midway
   - Guadalcanal campaign (heavy action)
   - Famous action: **Battle of Kolombangara** (July 13, 1943)
     - Night surface battle (Solomons)
     - Japanese force: Jintsuu + destroyers vs US cruisers/destroyers (Task Force 18)
     - **Jintsuu led torpedo attack** (aggressive, typical IJN doctrine)
     - Illuminated by US star shells (radar-detected)
     - **Concentrated fire from multiple US cruisers**
     - 6-inch and 5-inch shells hit Jintsuu repeatedly
     - Ship caught fire, exploded
     - **Sank in 30 minutes** (heroic but doomed)
     - **Captain Shimai killed on bridge** (fighting to end)
     - 482 killed, only 18 survivors
     - Torpedoes from Jintsuu/destroyers hit US cruisers (Leander, Honolulu, St. Louis damaged)
     - **Jintsuu's sacrifice enabled destroyer torpedo attacks**
   - Lost: July 13, 1943
   - Second Sendai-class lost
   - Most famous Sendai-class (heroic last stand)

3. **Naka** - Commissioned November 30, 1925
   - "Naka River"
   - Early war: Wake Island, Marshall Islands, Midway
   - Guadalcanal campaign
   - 1943: Truk (Caroline Islands - major Japanese base)
   - Famous action: **Operation Hailstone** (February 17-18, 1944)
     - US carrier raid on Truk (9 carriers, 500+ aircraft)
     - Massive surprise attack
     - Naka in harbor
     - **February 17, 1944**: Bombed by US carrier aircraft
     - Multiple bomb hits, torpedoes
     - Ship destroyed, sank in harbor
     - 240 killed
   - Lost: February 17, 1944 (technically 1944, but shortly after sisters)
   - Last Sendai-class lost

**Battle of Kolombangara (July 13, 1943) - Jintsuu:**

**Most Famous Sendai-Class Action:**

**Setup:**
- Location: Kolombangara Island, Solomons
- Date: Night of July 12-13, 1943
- **Japanese**: Light cruiser Jintsuu + 5 destroyers (Rear Admiral Izaki)
  - Mission: "Tokyo Express" supply run to Kolombangara
- **US**: Task Force 18 - 3 light cruisers (Honolulu, Helena, St. Louis) + HMNZS Leander + 10 destroyers (Rear Admiral Ainsworth)
  - Mission: Intercept Japanese

**The Battle:**
- **Night action** (typical for Solomons)
- **US advantage**: Radar (detected Japanese first)
- US opened fire first (illuminated with star shells)
- **Jintsuu's response**: Charged US line (aggressive IJN doctrine)
  - Led torpedo attack
  - Intended to get close, launch Long Lances
- **US concentrated fire on Jintsuu**:
  - Helena, Honolulu, St. Louis, Leander all fired on lead ship
  - 6-inch and 5-inch shells hit repeatedly
  - Jintsuu caught fire, explosions
  - **Captain Shimai killed on bridge** (commanding to end)
- **Jintsuu sank** after 30 minutes of battering
  - 482 killed (nearly entire crew)
  - Only 18 survivors
  - Ship exploded, broke apart

**Japanese Torpedoes:**
- Destroyers (and possibly Jintsuu before sinking) launched Type 93 Long Lances
- Long range (40,000 yards), wakeless, oxygen-fueled
- **Results**:
  - HMNZS Leander: Torpedo hit, heavily damaged (out of war for year)
  - USS Honolulu: Torpedo bow damage
  - USS St. Louis: Minor damage
- **US lost no ships but 3 cruisers damaged**

**Outcome:**
- **Tactical**: Draw (US stopped supply run, but 3 cruisers damaged)
- **Strategic**: US victory (Japanese forced to abandon Kolombangara eventually)
- **Jintsuu**: Heroic sacrifice (enabled destroyer torpedoes)
- Shows Japanese night fighting prowess AND vulnerability (light cruiser vs radar-equipped cruisers = death)

**Legacy:**
- Jintsuu's last stand remembered
- Captain Shimai honored (died in command)
- Shows IJN aggression (charge superior force)
- Also shows futility (light armor, no radar vs US technology)

**Battle of Empress Augusta Bay (November 2, 1943) - Sendai:**

**Second Sendai-Class Loss:**

**Setup:**
- Location: Empress Augusta Bay, Bougainville, Solomons
- Date: Night of November 1-2, 1943
- **Japanese**: Cruisers Sendai, Agano + 6 destroyers (Rear Admiral Omori)
  - Mission: Attack US invasion force at Bougainville
- **US**: Task Force 39 - 4 light cruisers + 8 destroyers (Rear Admiral Merrill)
  - Mission: Protect invasion force

**The Battle:**
- **Night action** (IJN preferred environment)
- **US radar** detected Japanese (again, technology advantage)
- Confused battle (both sides maneuvering)
- **Sendai engaged US cruisers**:
  - Hit by 6-inch shells from multiple US cruisers
  - Light armor penetrated easily
  - Fires, explosions
- **Sendai exploded** and sank
  - ~330 killed
  - Few survivors

**Outcome:**
- US victory (Japanese withdrew, invasion succeeded)
- Sendai lost
- Japanese failed to disrupt landings

**Shows Same Pattern:**
- Light cruiser vs US cruisers with radar = disaster
- Night battles no longer favored Japanese (radar negated advantage)
- 1943: US gaining technological and tactical upper hand

**Operation Hailstone (February 17-18, 1944) - Naka:**

**Final Sendai-Class Loss:**

**Setup:**
- **Truk Atoll**: Major Japanese base (Caroline Islands)
  - "Gibraltar of the Pacific"
  - Fleet anchorage, supply base
- **Operation Hailstone**: US carrier raid
  - 9 carriers (Fast Carrier Task Force)
  - 500+ aircraft
  - Surprise attack (February 17, 1944)

**The Attack:**
- Massive raid (like Pearl Harbor in reverse)
- Caught Japanese unprepared (thought Truk safe)
- Hundreds of aircraft bombed ships, facilities
- **Naka in harbor**:
  - No room to maneuver
  - Multiple bomb and torpedo hits
  - Ship destroyed, sank
  - 240 killed

**Results:**
- **Japanese losses**: ~40 ships sunk (cargo, cruisers, destroyers)
- 270+ aircraft destroyed
- Truk neutralized (could no longer serve as major base)
- **Naka**: Last Sendai-class lost

Same fast destroyer leader role - 35.5 knots, 7× 140mm guns, 8× Long Lance torpedoes. Sendai-class: Refined design, total sacrifice, remembered for Jintsuu's heroism.

**Historical Significance:** Sendai-class marked end of 5,500-ton light cruiser era. Final refinement of successful 1920s design. 3 ships (smallest class: budget constraints by mid-1920s). Shows evolution: Kuma (1920-1921) → Nagara (1922-1925) → Sendai (1924-1925) = continuous improvement over 5 years. Same philosophy throughout: Speed (35.5-36 knots) and torpedoes (Type 93 Long Lance) for destroyer flotilla leadership. By WWII, design obsolete: Light armor (2.5-inch) fatal in modern combat. 100% loss rate (all 3 destroyed 1943-1944) shows vulnerability. Jintsuu's Battle of Kolombangara (July 13, 1943): Most famous action - heroic charge vs superior US force, sank fighting, 482 killed, enabled destroyer torpedo hits. Showed IJN aggression and futility (light cruiser vs radar-equipped cruisers). Sendai at Empress Augusta Bay (November 2, 1943): Same pattern - night battle, US radar advantage, sank. Naka at Truk (February 17, 1944): Destroyed in harbor during Operation Hailstone (massive carrier raid). All lost within 7 months - concentrated attrition in 1943 (US gaining upper hand). Entire 5,500-ton series: 14 ships built 1920-1925, 13 lost, 1 survivor (Kitakami) - 92.9% loss rate. Contrast with US: Omaha-class (10 ships, all survived). Shows Japanese design compromises (speed over protection) and US advantages (radar, air power, better damage control). Sendai-class: Final evolution, complete sacrifice. Remembered especially for Jintsuu's last stand - symbol of IJN fighting spirit.
