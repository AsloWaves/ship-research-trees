**Earliest Modern Cruisers** - Takao-class (1888) were among Japan's first modern cruisers. 2 ships commissioned 1888-1889. British-built by Palmers. Tiny (1,720 tons), coal-fired, 18 knots. Two 10.2-inch guns (heavy for size). Sino-Japanese War veterans (1894-1895). Too old for Russo-Japanese War. Both scrapped 1911 (23 years service). Represented earliest Meiji-era naval modernization - Japan buying British ships to create modern navy. Note: Not same as WWII Takao-class heavy cruisers (1932).

**Key Specs:** 1,720/1,950 tons | 246ft | 18 kn | 2× 260mm + 6× 152mm | 51mm deck armor | Coal compound engines | 2 ships

**Innovation:** Among first modern Japanese cruisers (Meiji Restoration era). British-designed and built by Palmers Shipbuilding, Newcastle. Small (1,720 tons - corvette size by later standards). Two 10.2-inch guns (heavy armament for size - almost battleship caliber). Six 6-inch secondary guns. Protected cruiser concept. Coal-fired horizontal compound engines. 18 knots (adequate for 1880s). Fought Sino-Japanese War (1894-1895) - Japan's first modern naval victory. Too old for Russo-Japanese War (scrapped before). Both scrapped 1911 after 23 years service. Founded Japanese cruiser navy - from zero to modern fleet in one generation.

**Design:** Small protected cruiser hull (246 ft, 1,720 tons - very small). Two single 10.2-inch guns (heavy guns for small ship - one forward, one aft). Six single 6-inch guns (broadside). Two 47mm guns. Four 14-inch torpedo tubes. Protected cruiser armor: 2-inch deck over machinery/magazines, no belt armor. Coal-fired horizontal compound reciprocating engines. Two shafts. 18 knots. Good range (3,500 nm for size). Crew 220. British construction. 1880s technology - pre-dreadnought, pre-turbine, Victorian era.

**Names honored by reuse for more famous ships**

**Legacy:** Takao-class (1888) represented Japan's earliest modern cruiser navy. Two tiny British-built ships (1,720 tons) commissioned 1888-1889 during Meiji Restoration. Among first modern steel warships for Imperial Japanese Navy. Two 10.2-inch guns (heavy for size). Fought Sino-Japanese War (1894-1895) - Japan's first modern naval victory. Too old for Russo-Japanese War (1904-1905). Both scrapped 1911 after 23 years service. Founded Japanese cruiser tradition. Names later reused for famous WWII Takao-class heavy cruisers (1932). Original Takao and Maya: Victorian-era pioneers forgotten, but names remembered.

**Historical Significance:** Takao-class (1888) marked Japan's entry into modern naval power. Commissioned 1888-1889 just 20 years after Meiji Restoration (1868) - remarkably fast modernization. British-built because Japan couldn't yet build modern warships. Fought Sino-Japanese War (1894) - proved Japan had modern navy capable of defeating traditional Asian power (China). By time scrapped (1911), Japan building own modern warships, defeating European power (Russia, 1905), becoming recognized naval power. Evolution visible: Takao (1888, British-built, 1,720 tons, coal, 10.2-inch guns) → Takao (1932, Japanese-built, 13,000 tons, oil, 10× 8-inch guns, world-class) = 44 years transformation from student to master. Original Takao: Humble beginning. Later Takao: Great power achievement. Names survived because ships honored.
