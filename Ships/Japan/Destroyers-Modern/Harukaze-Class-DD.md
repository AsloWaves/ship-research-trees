**First Post-WWII Destroyers** - Harukaze-class were Japan's first destroyers built after WWII. 2 ships commissioned 1956. First warships for Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force (JMSDF, established 1954). Marked Japan's return to naval construction (11 years after WWII surrender). 2,340 tons, 30 knots, 3× 5-inch guns, ASW weapons. US equipment throughout (5-inch Mk 39 guns, SPS-6 radar, SQS-4 sonar, Mk 37 fire control). Modest size (post-war constraints, constitutional limitations, economic recovery). ASW-focused mission (Cold War, counter Soviet submarines). Both served 29-30 years (1956-1985/1986). Harukaze DD-101 (first JMSDF destroyer number). Yukikaze DD-102 (honored legendary WWII survivor destroyer). Foundation for modern JMSDF development - established patterns for future Japanese destroyers.

**Key Specs:** 1,700/2,340 tons | 344ft | 30 kn | 3× 127mm + ASW weapons | 2 ships | Both served 29-30 years

**Innovation:** **Historic firsts** - First destroyers built for Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force (JMSDF), first post-WWII Japanese warships (11 years after 1945 surrender). Japan's return to naval construction (Allied occupation ended 1952, rearmament gradual). **Post-war constraints**: Article 9 (Japanese Constitution - renounce war), economic limitations (recovering from WWII devastation), US supervision (training, equipment, doctrine). **Modest design**: 2,340 tons full (vs WWII destroyers 2,500-3,000 tons), 30 knots (adequate but not exceptional), minimal weapons (3× 5-inch guns vs WWII 6-8 guns). **US equipment throughout**: 5-inch Mk 39 guns (US single mounts), SPS-6 air search radar (US), SPS-5 surface search (US), SQS-4 sonar (US), Mk 37 fire control (US). American technology transfer (JMSDF trained by US Navy). **ASW-focused mission**: Cold War context (Soviet submarine threat), Japan's geographic position (choke points for Soviet fleet). Hedgehog ASW mortar, depth charges, ASW torpedoes. **Established pattern**: US equipment + Japanese hulls → gradual indigenization → modern JMSDF (indigenous designs 1970s+). Harukaze/Yukikaze: Humble beginnings, historic significance.

**Design:** Compact destroyer hull (344 ft, 2,340 tons full - small by modern standards). **Three 127mm (5-inch) Mk 39 guns**: US single mounts, one forward, two aft (superfiring). Mk 37 fire control system (US - radar-directed). Four 40mm Bofors AA guns (two twin mounts - WWII-era AA). **ASW armament**: Hedgehog ASW mortar forward (spigot mortar, 24 projectiles), depth charge racks aft, two triple 324mm torpedo tubes (Mk 32 - ASW homing torpedoes). **US sensors**: SPS-6 air search radar (mainmast), SPS-5 surface search (aft mast), SQS-4 hull-mounted sonar (bow dome). Simple mast structure (no complex electronics - 1950s era). Single funnel (steam plant). **Steam turbine propulsion**: Two oil-fired boilers, steam turbines, 30,000 shp, two shafts, 30 knots. Range 6,000 nm at 18 knots (good endurance). Crew 240 (manual systems, 1950s manning). Basic destroyer profile (functional, no frills). Painted JMSDF gray (light gray livery).

**Ships (2 built - both served full careers):**

1. **Harukaze (DD-101)** - Commissioned April 25, 1956
   - Lead ship, name means "Spring Wind"
   - **DD-101**: First JMSDF destroyer hull number (historic significance!)
   - Built by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Nagasaki
   - Laid down: December 20, 1954
   - Launched: September 20, 1955
   - Commissioned: April 25, 1956
   - Service:
     - Cold War ASW patrols (1956-1985)
     - Training missions (Japanese crews, US cooperation)
     - Fleet exercises (bilateral, multilateral)
     - Search and rescue operations
     - Diplomatic cruises (show Japanese flag, peaceful maritime nation)
   - Modernization 1960s:
     - Radar upgrades
     - Sonar improvements
     - Electronics updates
   - Decommissioned: March 31, 1985
   - **29 years service**
   - Scrapped 1985-1986
   - Historic ship (first post-WWII destroyer)

2. **Yukikaze (DD-102)** - Commissioned June 30, 1956
   - "Snow Wind"
   - **Honored legendary WWII destroyer name**:
     - Original Yukikaze (Kagero-class, 1939-1947): Most famous WWII Japanese destroyer
     - Fought entire war, survived EVERY major battle (Pearl Harbor, Guadalcanal, Philippine Sea, Leyte Gulf, etc.)
     - Only Kagero-class survivor (all 18 others lost!)
     - Transferred to Republic of China (Taiwan) 1947 as Dan Yang
     - New Yukikaze DD-102: Honored legacy (skill, luck, survival)
   - Built by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Nagasaki
   - Laid down: March 31, 1955
   - Launched: November 23, 1955
   - Commissioned: June 30, 1956
   - Service: Similar to Harukaze
     - ASW operations
     - Training
     - Fleet exercises
     - Disaster relief
   - Decommissioned: March 20, 1986
   - **30 years service** (longest of class)
   - Scrapped 1986
   - Legendary name carried forward

**Post-WWII Japan Context:**

**Surrender to Rearmament (1945-1956):**

**1945-1952 (Occupation):**
- August 15, 1945: Japan surrendered (WWII ended)
- Allied occupation (US-led, General Douglas MacArthur)
- Demilitarization: IJN dissolved, ships scrapped/transferred
- Article 9 (1947 Constitution): "Renounce war as sovereign right"
- No military forces allowed (initially)

**1950 (Korean War Begins):**
- June 25, 1950: North Korea invaded South Korea
- US forces deployed from Japan
- US realized: Need Japan to defend itself (free up US forces)
- August 1950: National Police Reserve created (75,000 men - proto-military)

**1952 (Independence Restored):**
- San Francisco Peace Treaty (September 8, 1951, effective April 28, 1952)
- Japan regained sovereignty
- US-Japan Security Treaty: US defense commitment, bases in Japan
- Police Reserve → Safety Forces (army/navy equivalents)

**1954 (JMSDF Established):**
- July 1, 1954: Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force officially established
- Constitutional basis: Self-defense allowed (not offensive war)
- Mission: Defend Japanese waters, support US alliance
- Equipment: US-supplied initially (destroyers, landing craft, etc.)

**1956 (Harukaze Commissioned):**
- First destroyers built in Japan since WWII
- Symbolic: Japan can build warships again (sovereignty, capability)
- But: Modest size, US equipment (gradual independence)

**US Equipment - Technology Transfer:**

**Why All-American Equipment:**
1. **US military aid**: Equipment provided/sold to Japan (Cold War ally)
2. **Standardization**: Work with US Navy (same equipment = interoperability)
3. **Proven technology**: US weapons tested, reliable
4. **Training**: US advisors familiar with equipment (transfer knowledge)
5. **Cost**: US production scale = cheaper than developing Japanese systems

**Equipment List:**
- **5-inch Mk 39 guns**: US single mount, Mk 37 fire control
- **40mm Bofors**: Swedish design, US production (WWII-era AA)
- **SPS-6 radar**: US air search (medium-range)
- **SPS-5 radar**: US surface search
- **SQS-4 sonar**: US hull-mounted sonar (active/passive)
- **Hedgehog**: British-designed, US-produced ASW mortar
- **Mk 32 torpedo tubes**: US ASW torpedo system

**Gradual Indigenization:**
- 1960s-1970s: Japan begins domestic production (license-built US equipment)
- 1980s: Japanese-designed systems (FCS-2 fire control, OPS-14 radar, etc.)
- 1990s-2000s: Fully indigenous designs (Aegis variants, etc.)
- Harukaze: First step in progression (all-US → mixed → all-Japanese)

**ASW Focus - Cold War Mission:**

**Soviet Submarine Threat:**
- USSR had large submarine fleet (200+ submarines 1960s)
- Pacific Fleet based Vladivostok (Soviet Far East)
- Regular patrols: Sea of Okhotsk, Sea of Japan, Pacific Ocean
- Nuclear submarines (1960s+): Strategic threat (ballistic missile submarines)

**Japanese Geographic Position:**
- **Choke points**: Three main straits connect Sea of Japan to Pacific
  1. Tsushima Strait (Korea-Japan)
  2. Tsugaru Strait (Hokkaido-Honshu)
  3. Soya Strait (Hokkaido-Sakhalin)
- Soviet submarines must transit these straits (shallow, narrow - vulnerable)
- JMSDF mission: Monitor, track, report Soviet submarine movements

**Harukaze ASW Capabilities:**
- **SQS-4 sonar**: Detect submarines underwater (active pings, passive listening)
- **Hedgehog**: ASW mortar (forward-throwing, 24 projectiles)
- **Depth charges**: Traditional ASW weapon (drop astern, explode at depth)
- **ASW torpedoes**: Homing torpedoes (Mk 32 tubes, Mk 44/46 torpedoes)
- **Tactics**: Patrol choke points, investigate contacts, coordinate with US Navy

**Effectiveness:**
- Harukaze/Yukikaze: Basic ASW capability (adequate 1950s-1960s)
- Later ships better (variable depth sonar, ASROC missiles, helicopters)
- But: Foundation for Japanese ASW expertise (training, tactics, doctrine)

**DD-101 Hull Number - Historic:**

**JMSDF Numbering System:**
- DD: Destroyer
- 101-199: Destroyers (sequential)
- First ship: DD-101 (Harukaze)
- Subsequent ships: DD-102 (Yukikaze), DD-103, DD-104, etc.

**Significance:**
- **DD-101 Harukaze**: First post-WWII Japanese destroyer
- **Historic moment**: 11 years after surrender, Japan building warships again
- **Symbol**: Japan's return to maritime power (peaceful, defensive)
- Current DD-101: Still in use (different ship, number reused - tradition)

**Yukikaze Name - Legendary Legacy:**

**Original Yukikaze (1939-1947):**
- Kagero-class destroyer (19 ships built)
- Commissioned: January 20, 1940
- **"Miracle Destroyer"**: Fought EVERY major WWII battle, survived ALL
  - Pearl Harbor attack (December 1941)
  - Wake Island
  - Battle of the Coral Sea (May 1942)
  - Battle of Midway (June 1942)
  - Guadalcanal campaign (1942-1943)
  - Battle of the Philippine Sea (June 1944)
  - Battle of Leyte Gulf (October 1944)
  - Operation Ten-Go (Yamato's final mission, April 1945)
  - Many others
- **Only Kagero-class survivor** (all 18 sisters lost!)
- **Luckiest Japanese destroyer** (superstition: "Yukikaze escort = survival")
- Post-war: Reparations to Republic of China (Taiwan)
- Renamed: **Dan Yang** (ROCS Dan Yang, DD-12)
- Served ROC Navy: 1947-1966 (longest-serving WWII destroyer!)
- Scrapped: 1970 (30 years service total)

**DD-102 Yukikaze (1956-1986):**
- Honored legendary name (skill, luck, survival)
- Carried forward proud tradition
- 30 years service (1956-1986 - also long career!)
- Modern JMSDF: Name retired (respect for original ship)

**29-30 Year Service - Longevity:**

**Long Careers:**
- Harukaze: 1956-1985 (29 years)
- Yukikaze: 1956-1986 (30 years)
- Excellent for 1950s destroyers (typical 20-25 years)

**Why So Long:**
1. **Quality construction**: Mitsubishi Heavy Industries (experienced, wartime yard)
2. **Moderate use**: Not frontline combat ships (peacetime patrols)
3. **Good maintenance**: JMSDF maintenance culture (excellent upkeep)
4. **Modernization**: Upgrades extended service lives (radar, electronics)
5. **Peacetime**: No battle damage, combat stress

**Comparison - Destroyer Lifespans:**
- US Fletcher-class (WWII): 20-30 years typical
- Soviet Skory-class (1950s): 20-25 years
- British Daring-class (1950s): 20-25 years
- **Harukaze-class: 29-30 years** (exceptional for size/era)
