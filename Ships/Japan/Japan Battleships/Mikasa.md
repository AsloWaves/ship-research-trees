**Togo's Flagship** - Mikasa was Admiral Togo's flagship at Battle of Tsushima (May 27-28, 1905) - most decisive naval battle in history. British-built pre-dreadnought battleship. Commissioned 1902. Four 12-inch guns, fourteen 6-inch guns. Led Japanese fleet to victory over Russia. Only pre-dreadnought battleship preserved as museum ship (Yokosuka, Japan). Most historically significant Japanese warship ever built.

**Key Specs:** 15,140/15,375 tons | 432ft | 18 kn | 4× 305mm + 14× 152mm | 229mm belt | 1 ship

**Innovation:** Most powerful Japanese battleship when built (1902). First modern Japanese capital ship. British design and construction (Vickers, Barrow-in-Furness). Embodied pre-dreadnought design philosophy - mixed battery of 12-inch, 6-inch, smaller guns. Admiral Togo's flagship at Tsushima - led fleet that destroyed Russian Baltic Fleet. Symbol of Japanese naval power and Meiji Restoration success. Only surviving pre-dreadnought battleship in world.

**Design:** Two twin 12-inch turrets (one forward, one aft). Fourteen 6-inch guns in casemates. Twenty 3-inch guns. Four submerged 18-inch torpedo tubes. Heavy armor (9-inch belt). Triple expansion steam engines. Coal-fired boilers. Typical pre-dreadnought layout. Ram bow. Two masts. Ornate decorations. British construction quality.

**Construction:**
- Builder: Vickers, Sons and Maxim, Barrow-in-Furness, UK
- Laid down: January 24, 1899
- Launched: November 8, 1900
- Commissioned: March 1, 1902
- Cost: £880,000 (British pounds)

**Service:**
- **1902-1904:** Training, fleet exercises, China station
- **1904-1905:** **Russo-Japanese War** flagship
- **1905-1912:** Training ship, reserve
- **1912-1921:** Coast defense, training
- **1921:** Decommissioned, designated memorial ship
- **1923:** Permanently preserved as museum ship

**Russo-Japanese War (1904-1905):**
**Background:**
- War between Japan and Russia over Korea and Manchuria
- First major war between Asian and European power
- Japan sought to establish itself as modern military power

**Battle of Port Arthur (February 8-9, 1904):**
- Japanese surprise attack on Russian Pacific Fleet
- Mikasa participated in bombardment
- Several Russian battleships damaged

**Battle of the Yellow Sea (August 10, 1904):**
- Attempted to prevent Russian fleet escape from Port Arthur
- Mikasa led battle line
- Heavy action, Mikasa hit by ~22 shells
- Russian flagship Tsesarevich damaged, fleet scattered
- Tactical Japanese victory

**Battle of Tsushima (May 27-28, 1905):**
*Most decisive naval battle in history*

**Background:**
- Russian Baltic Fleet sailed 18,000 miles to relieve Port Arthur
- Admiral Togo's Combined Fleet intercepted in Tsushima Strait
- **Forces:**
  - Japanese: 4 battleships, 8 cruisers, 21 destroyers
  - Russian: 8 battleships, 3 coastal battleships, 8 cruisers, 9 destroyers

**May 27, 1905 - The Battle:**
- **1:55 PM:** Russian fleet sighted
- **2:02 PM:** Admiral Togo famously signaled: **"The Empire's fate depends on this battle. Let every man do his utmost."**
- **2:05 PM:** Togo's famous "Crossing the T" maneuver
  - Turned battle line in front of Russian fleet
  - Concentrated all Japanese guns on leading Russian ships
- **2:08 PM:** Battle commenced at 6,400 yards
- **Mikasa led the line** - absorbed heavy fire
- Hit by ~30+ shells from Russian battleships
- Continued to fight and lead
- Superior Japanese gunnery devastated Russian fleet
- Russian flagship Knyaz Suvorov disabled
- One by one, Russian battleships destroyed or captured

**Results:**
- **Russian losses:**
  - 21 ships sunk
  - 6 ships captured
  - 4,380 killed
  - 5,917 captured
- **Japanese losses:**
  - 3 torpedo boats sunk
  - 117 killed
- **Most one-sided naval victory in history**
- Russia sued for peace (Treaty of Portsmouth)
- Japan emerged as world naval power

**Mikasa at Tsushima:**
- Hit by 32 shells (12-inch and 6-inch)
- Extensive damage but remained operational
- 113 crew killed, 100+ wounded
- Never left the line
- Led fleet throughout 2-day battle
- Symbol of Japanese victory

**Admiral Togo:**
- Commander of Combined Fleet
- "Nelson of the East"
- Tsushima victory made him national hero
- Mikasa was his flagship throughout war
- Famous signal: "Empire's fate depends on this battle"
- His flag flew from Mikasa's mainmast

**"Crossing the T":**
- Classic naval tactic executed at Tsushima
- Togo turned fleet in front of Russian column
- All Japanese guns could fire, only Russian lead ships could respond
- Devastating advantage
- Perfectly executed under fire
- Mikasa led the dangerous turn

**Post-War:**
**September 11, 1905 - Magazine Explosion:**
- Anchored at Sasebo after Tsushima victory
- Magazine explosion (cause unknown - possibly sabotage)
- Sank in harbor
- 339 crew killed
- Salvaged November 1905
- Repaired and returned to service 1906

**Preservation:**
- **1921:** Decommissioned under Washington Naval Treaty
- **1923:** Designated as memorial ship
- Preserved at Yokosuka as museum
- **1923:** Great Kanto Earthquake caused some damage
- **1945:** Survived WWII
- **1945-1952:** Neglected during occupation
- **1958-1961:** Major restoration
- **Still preserved today** - over 120 years old

**Historical Significance:**
1. **Tsushima Victory:** Most decisive naval battle in history
2. **Japan's emergence:** First Asian power to defeat European power
3. **Naval revolution:** Demonstrated importance of unified command, superior training
4. **Pre-dreadnought end:** Last major battle of pre-dreadnought era
5. **Togo's legacy:** Made Admiral Togo legendary naval hero
6. **National symbol:** Embodied Japan's rapid modernization

**Why Tsushima Was So One-Sided:**
1. **Japanese advantages:**
   - Superior gunnery training
   - Better ships (newer, maintained)
   - Fresh crews vs exhausted Russian crews
   - Home waters - knew terrain
   - Unified command under Togo
2. **Russian disadvantages:**
   - 18,000-mile voyage exhausted crews
   - Ships fouled and unmaintained
   - Poor morale
   - Inferior gunnery
   - Divided command structure

**Museum Ship Today:**
- Located at Yokosuka, Japan
- Fully preserved and restored
- **Only surviving pre-dreadnought battleship in world**
- **Only ship from Russo-Japanese War still existing**
- Over 300,000 visitors per year
- UNESCO Memory of the World Register
- National Historic Site of Japan

**Battle Damage at Tsushima:**
- 32 shell hits (mostly 12-inch)
- Funnel riddled with holes
- Masts damaged
- Bridge hit multiple times
- Casualties: 113 killed, 100+ wounded
- But remained in action throughout battle
- Showed toughness of pre-dreadnought design

**Legacy:** Mikasa is most historically significant Japanese warship. Admiral Togo's flagship at Tsushima - most decisive naval battle ever fought. Led "Crossing the T" maneuver that destroyed Russian Baltic Fleet. Absorbed heavy punishment but remained in action. Symbolized Japan's emergence as modern naval power. Preserved as only surviving pre-dreadnought battleship. Over 120 years old, still standing at Yokosuka. More important historically than even Yamato - Mikasa's victory shaped 20th century. Name means "Mount Mikasa" (Nara, Japan) - sacred mountain.
