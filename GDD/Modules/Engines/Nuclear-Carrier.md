## Historical Notes

USS Enterprise (CVN-65), commissioned 1961, was the world's first nuclear-powered aircraft carrier. Eight A2W reactors generated 280,000 SHP, enabling sustained 33+ knot speed and unlimited range. During the 1964 "Operation Sea Orbit," Enterprise circumnavigated the globe with nuclear cruiser escort - 30,565 miles in 64 days without refueling, demonstrating nuclear power's strategic value.

The Nimitz-class carriers (1975-2009) standardized nuclear carrier design with two A4W reactors producing 260,000 SHP total (exact output classified). During Operation Desert Storm (1991), USS Abraham Lincoln operated continuously for months without refueling, launching 3,000+ sorties. The operational tempo impossible with conventional propulsion demonstrated nuclear power's tactical advantage.

Nuclear propulsion's greatest advantage is eliminating the logistics tail. A conventional carrier requires fleet oilers every few days for fuel replenishment. Nuclear carriers only need food and aviation fuel, reducing underway replenishment by 60%. During extended operations, this logistics advantage compounds - the carrier can remain on station while conventional escorts must detach for refueling.

The Ford-class carriers (2017+) use new A1B reactors with 25% more electrical power (300+ MW total vs. 64 MW Nimitz) and 50-year core life eliminating mid-life refueling. The increased electrical power enables electromagnetic aircraft launch systems (EMALS) and advanced sensors. The 50-year core matches expected hull life, simplifying operations.

Only the United States and France operate nuclear carriers (Charles de Gaulle with K15 reactors). The enormous cost and complexity limit nuclear carriers to superpowers. However, for sustained power projection operations, nuclear propulsion remains unmatched - a nuclear carrier can operate at high tempo for months, while conventional carriers must moderate operations to conserve fuel.
