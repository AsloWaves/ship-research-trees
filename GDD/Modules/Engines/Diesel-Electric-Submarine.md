## Description

Standard submarine propulsion combining diesel engines for surface operation and electric motors for submerged running. Diesel engines drive generators that charge batteries and power electric motors. This dual system allows submarines to cruise efficiently on the surface while retaining underwater capability. Dominated submarine design from WWI through the nuclear era.

## Historical Notes

Diesel-electric propulsion defined submarine design from WWI through the 1950s. German U-boats of both World Wars used this system - U-boats typically spent 95% of time surfaced, diving only for attacks or evasion. The combination of excellent surface range with underwater capability made them effective commerce raiders.

American fleet submarines like Gato-class (1941) perfected diesel-electric design with four diesel engines producing 5,400 SHP surface, achieving 20 knots. Submerged, electric motors provided 2,740 SHP for 9 knots sprint or 48+ hours at 2 knots. The 11,000nm range allowed Pacific operations from Pearl Harbor to Japanese home waters.

German Type VII U-boats used diesel-electric drive throughout WWII. Surface cruising at 10 knots gave 8,500nm range - enough to reach American coast from French bases. The main tactical limitation was battery endurance; aggressive attacks requiring high submerged speed could drain batteries in under an hour, forcing the submarine to surface in dangerous waters.

The snorkel (Dutch invention, 1940) extended submerged endurance dramatically by allowing diesel operation at periscope depth. Late-war U-boats with snorkels could remain submerged for weeks, running diesels at shallow depth to charge batteries. However, snorkeling was slow (6 knots maximum) and still detectable by radar.

Post-WWII diesel-electric submarines incorporated improved batteries and streamlined hulls (GUPPY conversions). American Tang-class (1951) and British Oberon-class (1959) represented the peak of conventional submarine design before nuclear power. Modern diesel-electric submarines like German Type 212 use similar principles with vastly improved batteries and AIP (Air Independent Propulsion) systems.
