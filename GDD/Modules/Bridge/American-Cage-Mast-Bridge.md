## Description

Distinctive lattice mast structure supporting the bridge on American "Standard-type" battleships and early cruisers. The cage mast became the iconic symbol of the pre-radar US Navy. Designed to resist shell damage through open construction while providing elevated spotting positions, it proved both innovative and problematic.

## The Cage Mast Problem

### Vibration Issues
Fundamental flaw discovered:
- Mast vibrates at high speed
- 20+ knots causes severe oscillation
- Rangefinding accuracy reduced
- Observer sickness common
- Fire control degraded

### Whipping Effect
Battle damage concern:
- Multiple shells cause cumulative stress
- Entire structure can fail
- Falls across deck
- Catastrophic damage potential
- Never actually tested in combat

### Battle of Jutland Lessons (1916)
British experience influenced USN:
- Director fire control superior
- Cage masts obsolete concept
- Tripod masts more stable
- US began transition
- WWII modernizations

## Historical Notes

The cage mast represents American naval innovation at both its best and worst. The concept was theoretically sound - an open structure that shells would pass through without exploding. In practice, it created more problems than it solved. The vibration issues alone made long-range gunnery (the mast's primary purpose) less effective. By 1916, British experience at Jutland showed that director fire control from stable platforms was superior. The US Navy began transitioning to tripod masts, but most Standard-type battleships kept their cage masts until major rebuilds in the 1930s or post-Pearl Harbor. Today, the cage masts of USS Arizona remain visible above Pearl Harbor, an enduring symbol of both American innovation and the day that changed naval warfare forever.
