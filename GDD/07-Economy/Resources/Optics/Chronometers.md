## Lore

Marine chronometers represented centuries of horological precision engineering—spring-driven timepieces capable of maintaining accuracy within seconds per day despite temperature changes, humidity, and constant ship motion. Without accurate time, celestial navigation became guesswork. A chronometer error of just four seconds translated to one nautical mile of positioning error, potentially meaning the difference between making landfall safely or running aground on uncharted reefs.

British chronometer makers like Hamilton and Thomas Mercer dominated the prewar market, their instruments carried aboard warships and merchant vessels worldwide. When war began, American manufacturers like Hamilton Watch Company ramped up production, eventually producing thousands of marine chronometers monthly for both US and Allied navies. German U-boats relied on chronometers from A. Lange & Söhne and other traditional makers, with replacement units becoming increasingly scarce as the war progressed.

The importance of chronometers extended beyond navigation. Fire control systems required precise time synchronization for salvo timing, while radio navigation systems needed accurate timekeeping for signal interpretation. When HMS Prince of Wales and Repulse sortied against Japanese invasion forces, their navigators relied on chronometers to maintain position during radio silence. Japanese surface raiders operating in the Indian Ocean carried multiple chronometers as backup, knowing that chronometer failure thousands of miles from repair facilities could end their cruise prematurely.
